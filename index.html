Ultrasound-Guided Nerve Blocks Improve Success Rate of Closed Reduction of Colles' Fractures

A Critical Appraisal & Infographic Summary for UK Emergency Medicine

The Clinical Question (PICO)

Population: 238 adult patients in 4 Danish Emergency Departments with Colles' fractures requiring closed reduction.

Intervention: Ultrasound-guided nerve block of the radial and median nerves (US Group).

Comparison: Haematoma block (HB Group).

Outcome: Primary Outcome: Satisfactory fracture reduction on post-procedure X-ray (based on set radiological criteria). Secondary Outcomes: Need for subsequent surgical correction, self-reported pain (NRS), and time in ED.

Study Design

  • Multicentre (4 Danish hospitals)
  • Parallel-group
  • Open-label
  • Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT)
  • 1:1 randomisation
  • Intention-to-treat analysis

Who Was Studied?

238 patients with Colles' fracture

117 randomised to US, 121 to HB

Median age: 64 years

~80% female (typical for this fracture)

Exclusions:

Allergy, infection, multitrauma, inability to consent.

Crossover & Failures

A key finding was the block failure rate:

12% of the US Group (15 patients) had a failed block and required a rescue haematoma block.

This crossover dilutes the true effect of the intervention.

Primary Outcome: Satisfactory Fracture Reduction

The US-guided block group had a significantly higher rate of successful radiological reduction.

US-GUIDED BLOCK (n=117)

62%

(73 patients)

HAEMATOMA BLOCK (n=121)

40%

(49 patients)

p = 0.01

This difference is statistically significant.

Secondary Outcome: Subsequent Surgical Correction

Fewer patients in the US group went on to require surgical intervention at follow-up (approx. 10 days).

US-GUIDED BLOCK (n=117)

52%

(61 patients)

HAEMATOMA BLOCK (n=121)

66%

(80 patients)

p = 0.03

This difference is also statistically significant.

Secondary Outcomes: Pain & Time

This is where the results are nuanced. The US block was not a simple win for patient comfort or speed.

Pain DURING BLOCK

The US block was less painful to perform.

NRS 3 (US) vs 6 (HB)

p < 0.01

Pain DURING REDUCTION

There was NO difference in pain during the manipulation.

NRS 5 (US) vs 4 (HB)

p = 0.21

Time to Reduction

The US block took 20 minutes longer from block to reduction.

45 min (US) vs 25 min (HB)

p < 0.01

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Pragmatic multicentre design.
  • Answers a clinically relevant ED question.
  • Used operators with varied experience, reflecting real-world practice.
  • Hard primary outcome (radiological) and important secondary outcome (surgery).

Limitations

  • Open-label design: High risk of performance bias. Doctors knew the block and might have tried harder (or given up earlier) based on their bias.
  • Subjective Outcome: Assessors had "notably high" inter-observer variability (only 76% agreement), questioning the primary outcome's reliability.
  • Crossover: 12% failure rate of the US block dilutes the findings.
  • No Pain Benefit: The surprising lack of pain benefit during reduction is a major finding.
  • Time Delay: A 20-minute delay is operationally significant in a busy ED.

Clinical Bottom Line

For Colles' fractures, an ultrasound-guided median and radial nerve block resulted in better radiological reduction and a lower rate of subsequent surgery compared to a haematoma block.

However, this came at the cost of a 20-minute procedural delay and, critically, offered no improvement in patient-reported pain during the reduction itself. The study's open-label design is a major limitation.

Potential OSCE Questions

Examiner: "This study found ultrasound-guided blocks gave a better reduction, but didn't reduce pain during the manipulation. How do you explain this finding?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Acknowledge the Finding: "This is a key finding of the paper. While the US block was less painful to perform, it didn't improve pain scores during the reduction itself."
  2. Hypothesise the Mechanism: "The most likely explanation is that the US block (of median and radial nerves) provides superior motor blockade and muscle relaxation of the forearm."
  3. Contrast the Blocks: "A haematoma block primarily provides sensory anaesthesia at the fracture site, but does little to relax the muscles that are in spasm and resisting the reduction. The US block, by relaxing these muscles, likely allowed the operator to achieve a better reduction with less force, even if the deep periosteal pain from the fracture wasn't blocked any more effectively."
  4. Conclusion: "So, the benefit seen in the primary outcome (better reduction) was likely due to improved muscle relaxation, not superior analgesia."

Examiner: "The authors state this was an 'open-label' trial. What does this mean, and how could it have biased the results?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Define Open-Label: "An open-label trial means that both the clinicians and the patients were aware of which treatment was being administered. It was not blinded."
  2. State the Main Risk: "This introduces a significant risk of performance bias."
  3. Explain the Bias: "Because the doctor performing the reduction knew which block the patient had, their actions may have been unconsciously biased. For example, if a doctor believes US blocks are superior, they might have tried harder or for longer to achieve a perfect reduction in that group. Conversely, in the haematoma block group, they might have had a lower threshold for failure and given up sooner, listing the patient for surgery. This bias would artificially improve the results of the US block group."
  4. Link to Outcome: "This is especially problematic as the primary outcome (satisfactory reduction) was already noted to have high inter-observer variability, making it susceptible to this kind of bias."

Examiner: "This study showed a 20-minute procedural delay for the ultrasound group. How does this finding affect your decision to use this technique in your busy ED?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Acknowledge the Barrier: "The 20-minute median delay is a significant operational consideration for any busy Emergency Department. It represents a cost in terms of staff time and patient flow."
  2. Weigh the Trade-off: "This delay must be weighed against the study's main findings. The US block group had a 14% absolute reduction in surgical intervention (66% vs 52%)."
  3. Frame the Decision: "The decision is an operational trade-off: is investing an extra 20 minutes of skilled staff time in the ED worth a 14% reduction in the number of patients requiring a theatre slot and anaesthetic team later on? In most systems, saving theatre time is a major benefit."
  4. Personal/Dept. Context: "Therefore, while the time delay is a barrier, the potential to reduce surgical rates makes it a very compelling technique. Its feasibility would depend on departmental resources, sonography skills, and patient flow, but it highlights a 'spend to save' scenario where a small time investment in the ED could lead to much larger savings downstream."
